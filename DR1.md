# Integrated Computational Pipeline for Antibody Design

**Therapeutic antibodies dominate the top‐selling biologics (5 of the current top 10 drugs are monoclonals【72†L219-L228】)**, yet discovering high-affinity, developable antibodies remains slow and empirical. Recent advances in machine learning and generative models offer a paradigm shift. A seamless *in silico* pipeline would **generate candidate sequences**, **predict their structures**, **model antigen binding**, and **filter by developability** to accelerate design. Many specialized tools now exist for each step, but no single system handles all tasks end-to-end. Here we summarize key models and their data/architectures, chart the unmet needs in antibody discovery, and propose how to assemble these tools into an automated pipeline. The goal is a forward-looking framework that captivates researchers with concrete capabilities and clear value for stakeholders. 

## Sequence Generators: Language Models and Diffusers

- **AB-Gen (Jian et al., 2023)** – A GPT-2 based transformer trained on ~75 million human CDR-H3 sequences (OAS database)【78†L343-L352】, fine-tuned via reinforcement learning to propose novel CDRH3 loops optimized for multiple properties (specificity, viscosity, clearance, immunogenicity)【78†L343-L352】. *Inputs:* CDRH3 length (12–14 aa); *Outputs:* CDRH3 sequences with enhanced predicted properties. *Architecture:* 8-layer GPT (∼6M parameters) as prior model, same as RL agent【78†L343-L352】. *Limitation:* Focuses only on CDRH3 (not full variable region), and RL training is compute-intensive. 

- **AntiBERTy (Ruffolo et al., 2021)** – A transformer-based language model trained on 558 million antibody sequences【34†L64-L72】. It produces embeddings that correlate with experimental affinity: clusters antibodies by maturation/affinity in latent space. *Input:* Heavy+light sequences; *Output:* Embeddings. *Use:* Characterizing natural repertoire and guiding evolution, not direct generation. *Limitation:* Passive model – does not generate new sequences, and requires large data.

- **IgBERT / IgT5 (Olsen et al., 2024)** – Two large antibody-specific LMs trained on ~2.2 billion unpaired and 2 million paired heavy/light sequences【35†L178-L187】. *Purpose:* Sequence recovery, expression and binding prediction tasks. These models use BERT- and T5-style architectures. They achieved **state-of-art performance** on tasks relevant to expression and affinity【35†L178-L187】. *Disadvantage:* Very large models requiring massive compute; they model native-like sequences but do not generate antigen-specific variants out of the box.

- **IgLM (Ruffolo et al., 2023)** – An “infilling” language model trained on 558 million paired sequences (OAS heavy/light)【40†L318-L326】. It can mask and regenerate CDR loops (or entire sequences) under chain/species context. *Input:* Incomplete sequence (e.g. masked CDR); *Output:* Completed sequence. *Result:* Capable of designing new antibodies with better developability profiles. *Limitation:* Requires context windows; training on >10^8 sequences demands heavy resources【40†L318-L326】. 

- **IgCraft (Greenig et al., 2025)** – A unified generative framework (Bayesian Flow Networks) for **paired human antibody sequences**【58†L53-L62】. IgCraft handles multiple tasks in one model: unconditional sampling, sequence inpainting, inverse folding (seq→structure), and CDR grafting. It enforces “humanness” by training on natural repertoires, achieving **state-of-the-art grafting performance** (human-like CDR insertion)【58†L53-L62】. *Input/Output:* Depends on task (e.g. scaffold sequence in/out). *Limitation:* Restricted to human germline space; no explicit antigen conditioning.

- **IgBlend (Malherbe & Uçar, 2024)** – A *multimodal* antibody LLM that jointly models sequence and 3D structure. Trained on ~4 million antibody structures and 200 million sequences【56†L15-L24】, IgBlend embeds both sequence and backbone coordinates. It outperforms prior models on sequence recovery, CDR editing, and inverse folding benchmarks【56†L15-L24】. *Input:* (Optionally) incomplete sequence + reference backbone; *Output:* sequence completing that structure. *Uniqueness:* Integrates physical structure information to improve generative capability. *Limitation:* Extremely large training data; mainly tested on benchmark tasks (still new to practical design).

- **IgGM (Wang et al., 2025)** – A *foundational design model* for generating *antigen-specific* antibodies and nanobodies. IgGM simultaneously outputs a full antibody sequence **and its predicted antibody–antigen complex structure** given an epitope【60†L189-L197】. Internally it uses a pretrained sequence LM and a feature module to identify epitope-relevant features, then a network that outputs sequence + 3D complex. *Goal:* De novo design conditioned on target. *Limitation:* Complex model (currently a poster abstract); performance beyond *in silico* remains to be seen.

- **PALM (He et al., 2023)** – Pre-trained Antibody generative Language Model. It focuses on SARS-CoV-2: trained on large antibody datasets (OAS, CoV-AbDab, structural CDR databases) using a Roformer architecture【38†L83-L92】. PALM can generate heavy-chain CDR3 sequences conditioned on viral epitopes. It also includes a separate “A2binder” LM to predict ACE2 binding of generated antibodies. *Limitation:* Specialized to SARS-CoV-2 CDRs; not a general pipeline component.

- **AbX (Zhu et al., 2024)** – A score-based *diffusion model* jointly for antibody sequence and structure【52†L25-L34】. AbX incorporates pre-trained PLM priors (for evolutionary plausibility) and additional loss terms for physical (van der Waals) and geometric constraints. It operates in continuous time to model both discrete sequences and SE(3) coordinates【52†L25-L34】. *Input:* random noise; *Output:* antibody sequence + structure. *Results:* Outperforms prior methods on sequence/structure accuracy and binding affinity in tests【52†L25-L34】. *Limitation:* Requires careful tuning of constraints; diffusion models are compute-heavy.

## Structure Prediction and Binding Design

- **IgFold (Ruffolo et al., 2022)** – A deep model for rapid antibody structure prediction【31†L80-L87】. It uses a pre-trained antibody sequence LM (ESM-2) plus an SE(3)-equivariant graph network to predict Fv backbone in <25 seconds【31†L80-L87】. *Input:* paired heavy+light sequences; *Output:* 3D backbone + side-chains. IgFold achieves accuracy comparable to or better than AlphaFold on antibody loops, at ~100× speedup【31†L80-L87】. *Limitation:* Structural accuracy is high, but it focuses on structure only – it does not generate new sequences or consider antigens. 

- **ABodyBuilder3 (Abanades et al., 2024)** – The latest antibody structure model combining AlphaFold’s architecture with language-model embeddings【49†L147-L155】. It uses ProtT5 embeddings of the variable-region sequence fed into an AlphaFold-like invariant point attention network. ABodyBuilder3 achieves *new state-of-art accuracy on CDR loop modeling* by leveraging LM embeddings and refined relaxation【49†L147-L155】. *Output:* All-atom variable-region structure + per-residue confidence (pLDDT). *Limitation:* Computationally intensive like AlphaFold, but open-source. 

- **AbFlex (Jeon et al., 2024)** – An antigen-specific CDR design model【47†L106-L114】. AbFlex takes an antigen–antibody complex structure (wild-type antibody) and designs new CDR loops. It uses an equivariant graph neural network and a novel “flexible CDR definition” augmentation to learn from scarce complex data. Results show *>38% of designs have better predicted binding energy than wild-type*【47†L106-L114】. *Output:* New CDR sequences + structures that target the given antigen. *Limitation:* Requires known antigen structure and starting antibody; only designs loops (not whole sequence). 

- **DiffAb (Luo et al., 2022)** – A diffusion-based generative model for **antigen-specific CDR design**【67†L23-L32】. DiffAb jointly models CDR sequences and structures conditioned on a target antigen. It is the first DL method explicitly generating antibodies against a given antigen structure. *Input:* antigen coordinates; *Output:* candidate CDR sequences + 3D conformations. It serves as a “Swiss army knife” (co-design, sequence design, optimization)【67†L23-L32】. *Limitation:* Early model (NeurIPS 2022); diffusion over both sequence and structure is powerful but compute-intensive and trained on limited complex data.

- **RFDiffusion (Bennett et al., 2025)** – A fine-tuned RoseTTAFold diffusion network for antibody design. By optimizing *de novo* (from backbone noise) in the presence of an epitope, RFdiffusion can **generate single-domain (VHH) binders, scFvs, and full antibodies** targeting specified epitopes with atomic precision【64†L101-L110】. It requires yeast-display or similar follow-up; e.g. in the study, initial designs had moderate affinity, but *OrthoRep* evolution yielded nanomolar binders【64†L112-L121】. *Output:* designed antibody heavy/light chains and predicted complex. *Limitation:* Still needs experimental maturation; not yet a turnkey prediction of high-affinity binders solely *in silico*. 

- **AbDockGen (Jin et al., 2022)** – A hierarchical equivariant graph-network model for **paired design and docking**【45†L12-L21】. AbDockGen conditions on an antigen epitope to sequentially generate antibody paratope residues and simultaneously “fold and dock” them via force-prediction. Its Hierarchical Structure Refinement Network (HSRN) uses equivariant message-passing at each step to refine the binding interface【45†L12-L21】. *Input:* antigen epitope structure; *Output:* antibody paratope sequence + 3D coordinates. *Disadvantage:* Currently demonstrated on epitopes and paratopes, with performance evaluated by docking scores; practical end-to-end affinity not yet validated in vitro.

## Developability and Humanization Tools

Beyond binding, lead antibodies must be developable. Important applications include **immunogenicity prediction**, **stability**, **solubility**, **aggregation propensity**, and **humanization**. 

- **BioPhi (Waight et al., 2022)** – A *platform* for antibody humanization and humanness scoring【62†L216-L224】. It includes *Sapiens*, a deep-learning humanizer trained on OAS data, which can replace nonhuman framework residues to produce human-like sequences at scale (matching expert results on 177 test mAbs)【62†L216-L224】. It also includes *OASis*, an interpretable 9-mer-based humanness score that separates human vs non-human sequences and correlates with clinical immunogenicity【62†L216-L224】. *Usage:* Filter or modify sequences to reduce immunogenicity risk. *Limitation:* Assumes alignment to human repertoire; it does not predict functional properties beyond “humanness”.

- **Biophysical Property Predictors:** Machine learning models (often gradient boosting or neural nets) have been developed to predict expression yield, aggregation, and other developability metrics from sequence or sequence+structure. For example, models trained on high-throughput measurements have shown some success in predicting solubility and stability【68†L19-L23】. These can serve as **filters** in a pipeline. *Limitation:* Data for such models is limited and biased; predictions are approximate, so experimental validation remains essential.

## Applications and Current Gaps

Therapeutic antibody discovery involves many tasks: antigen target specification, epitope identification, de novo sequence generation, affinity maturation, developability optimization, and more. **Computational advances** have begun to impact each step. For example, homology modeling and docking have long aided engineering【69†L1-L4】, and modern PLMs can explore sequence space far beyond natural repertoires【68†L13-L22】. Structure prediction (AlphaFold2, IgFold) has made modeling the antibody fold routine. However, significant gaps remain. Key applications *still bottleneck progress*:

- **Epitope-specific design:** Most generation models produce generic antibodies or CDRs. Very few (DiffAb, AbDockGen, IgGM, RFDiffusion) explicitly condition on an antigen epitope【67†L23-L32】【64†L101-L110】. Bridging sequence design with target binding is critical for therapeutic leads.  
- **Multi-chain pairing:** Many methods generate only heavy chain CDRs (CDR3) or treat chains independently. Full VH–VL pairing (or heavy+light design) is underdeveloped.  
- **Developability multi-objective optimization:** Current models rarely enforce stability, solubility, and immunogenicity concurrently. Often a novel binder must then be “fixed” later. As Zheng *et al.* note, balancing binding affinity with developability is a major challenge【68†L19-L23】.  
- **Limited data for binding:** Training data for antibody–antigen complexes is small (~7,000 structures)【42†L413-L422】. Diffusion models and RL partly mitigate this by pretraining on large sequence databases【52†L25-L34】, but truly accurate affinity prediction models are still few.  
- **No unified pipeline:** As a review puts it, *“full in silico design is not yet viable”*, though the pieces are falling into place【73†L75-L83】. We have excellent sequence generators, fast structure predictors, and even docking algorithms, but no end-to-end architecture yet.

## Proposed End-to-End Pipeline

An effective workflow would **chain specialized models**, using each tool’s strengths to address the gaps above:

- **1. Antigen/Epitope Input:**  Begin with the target (antigen or epitope structure). If epitope is known, tools like **AbDockGen** or **RFDiffusion** can directly use it【64†L101-L110】. If only antigen sequence is known, predict its structure (AlphaFold/IgFold on antigen).
- **2. Sequence Generation:** Use a generative model to propose antibody sequences. For broad diversity, models like **IgCraft** or **AB-Gen** can sample human-like sequence space. For targeted design, **IgGM** or **PALM-H3** (with antigen context) could generate CDRs oriented to the epitope. Advanced multi-task models like IgBlend can “reconstruct” diverse sequences consistent with partial designs.
- **3. Structure Modeling:** Predict each candidate’s 3D structure quickly (e.g. **IgFold**【31†L80-L87】 or **ABodyBuilder3**【49†L147-L155】). These models input the proposed VH/VL sequence and output coordinates, enabling structural assessment.
- **4. Binding Assessment:** Dock or co-design with the antigen. For example, feed the antibody structure + antigen into **RFdiffusion** (which refines binders) or use **AbDockGen** to iteratively dock the CDRs【45†L12-L21】. Alternately, use AlphaFold Multimer or specialized docking (HADDOCK) to score binding. This yields a binding affinity estimate or complex model.
- **5. Developability Filter:** Evaluate each candidate’s biophysical properties. Apply tools like **BioPhi/Sapiens** for humanization【62†L216-L224】 and predictors for solubility and stability. Discard or redesign sequences failing criteria. 
- **6. Optimization Loop:** Top candidates can be iteratively refined. ML-based **affinity maturation** (e.g. applying RL rewards, or running a directed-evolution *Santiago* model) can improve binding while retraining sequence models. In practice, this step may loop with lab experiments (e.g. yeast display followed by retraining the generative model). As Bennett *et al.* showed, *in silico* designs can be matured via OrthoRep to yield single-digit nM binders【64†L112-L121】.

This envisioned **“AI-driven pipeline”** mirrors the idea of an *Antibody Design Agent*【70†L48-L58】: a collaborative system of specialized ML models and robotics. For example, one model could focus on sequence generation, another on structure, another on developability scoring, all orchestrated by an agent that balances their outputs【70†L47-L56】. Sequences pass through sub-agents – an expression/solubility agent, a binding agent – before final selection【70†L48-L57】. Such an autonomous cycle, supplemented by high-throughput assays, could iteratively refine antibody candidates to optimal multi-objective profiles【70†L48-L57】【70†L84-L92】.

## Value Proposition for Stakeholders

A validated end-to-end platform would **transform discovery**. For pharmaceutical companies and biotech startups, it means dramatically shorter timelines and lower costs: *“autonomously balancing multiple developability parameters”* could reduce reliance on trial-and-error experimentation【70†L84-L92】. In practical terms, AI-driven design may cut months or years off lead development and focus lab resources on only the most promising candidates. As experts note, computational methods **“provide faster results than arduous experimental approaches”**【72†L225-L233】. 

For investors and partners, the pipeline’s outputs (high-affinity, humanized antibody leads ready for expression) are a compelling asset. For patients, the promise is new therapies reaching clinic sooner with fewer side-effects (via built-in humanness and stability filters). If this “antibody AI agent” succeeds, it could *“deliver safer and more effective biologics at scale”*【70†L91-L94】. In sum, integrating these cutting-edge tools addresses current gaps and aligns with industry trends: as one review observes, *“we move towards a time… where it is possible to accurately model most antibodies and antigens, [unlocking] vast potential for true computational antibody design”*【73†L75-L83】.
